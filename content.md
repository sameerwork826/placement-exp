# 📄 **Interview Experience – Sameer Nandesh Wanjari (IDD Physics, IIT BHU)**

---

## **Mastercard Consulting – Analyst**

The process started with a GD round where multiple topics were given such as India’s current diplomatic situation, big fat Indian weddings, and whether India should host the Olympics. My topic was India’s diplomatic situation, and the discussion went well overall.

In the first technical round, the interviewer went quite deep into my ML projects mentioned in my resume. There was a discussion on one of my case studies, followed by a puzzle whose answer involved an infinite GP. I was also asked some Python-related questions like the difference between NumPy arrays and Python lists, and a question on GIL which I skipped since I wasn’t confident. I was able to answer most of the questions correctly.

Despite a good round, I never heard back for the next stage. This experience made me realize that shortlisting is not always predictable and depends on factors like panel preferences, timing, and overall impression.

---

## **HiLabs – Data Scientist**

The first round itself was a DSA round where I was asked to screen share and solve a problem on an online compiler in Python. I was able to reach a working solution but could not optimize it to the expected time complexity within the given time.

I was rejected after this round. From peers, I got to know that the process further included an ML round and an HR round. This experience highlighted the importance of strong DSA and optimization skills even for data science roles.

---

## **CBA (Commonwealth Bank of Australia) – AI Engineer / Data Scientist**

This was the role I was primarily targeting and had prepared extensively for.

In the first round, I was asked questions on Git/GitHub, DSA (sorting and DP), and my projects. There was a deep discussion on vector databases like ChromaDB, similarity search, and HNSW indexing. I was also asked SQL questions orally and core Python OOP concepts like abstraction and encapsulation.

The second round was more of an ML system design discussion based on my background. I was asked how I would help improve my family’s school business. I proposed multiple ideas including AI-based marketing, building an application for school operations, and integrating an LLM chatbot for student-related queries. There was also an in-depth discussion on my loan risk prediction internship, particularly around the use of LLMs and handling PII data. The interviewer also discussed how their organization uses in-house models instead of APIs, which led to a broader discussion about model deployment and company strategies.

The third round was an HR and culture fit round. I was asked behavioral questions about achievements, failures, and situations involving conflicts with seniors. I mostly answered using my POR experiences, although at one point I was asked to shift focus to academic examples. The round went on for quite long and overall felt very positive.

Despite performing well across all rounds and being confident about my chances, I was not selected. This was a major learning experience about the unpredictability of final decisions.

---

## **InfoEdge – Data Scientist**

The first round was extremely intensive and focused heavily on theory. It started with linear algebra concepts such as PCA, covariance matrices, eigenvalues, and then moved on to statistical topics like MLE and probability distributions.

The discussion further covered machine learning concepts like SVM, KNN, ROC-AUC, precision-recall, and curse of dimensionality. I was also asked deep theoretical questions such as derivation of PCA and differences between SVD and PCA, along with a DSA problem focusing on optimization.

I gave this interview later in the day as I was prioritizing other companies. I did not hear back after this round. Looking back, prioritization and timing may have played a role here.

---

## **BlackRock – Data Analyst / Data Science**

In the first round, the discussion was mainly centered around my projects. There was a detailed conversation on my loan prediction project, including feature selection and BFSI-related concepts like NPA, DPD, and credit systems. The interviewer also asked about precision-recall trade-offs from a business perspective.

We also discussed the use of LLMs in finance, including data privacy concerns and approaches like anonymization and RAG pipelines. The conversation was quite engaging and lasted around 50–60 minutes.

However, since I was one of the last candidates to be interviewed, I did not get a chance to appear for the next round as the company had already finalized candidates. This taught me not to over-prioritize one company at the expense of others.

---

## **LEK Consulting – Analyst (Selected)**

This was the most chaotic yet rewarding experience of my placement journey due to overlapping interviews and connectivity issues, but I somehow managed to complete all rounds.

In the first round, I was given a case study on product cannibalization. The task was to analyze whether launching a new product would increase overall profits or lead to losses. The evaluation was based on both quantitative and qualitative reasoning, and the approach mattered a lot.

The second round involved two panel members and started with HR questions, followed by a case study on a merger and acquisition scenario. I was asked what metrics and factors I would consider while analyzing the acquisition, including identifying red flags and growth opportunities.

The third round was conducted by a senior manager and was more of a fit check. I was asked a guesstimate question on the number of traffic signals in a city and some general HR questions about my understanding of the company and my future plans.

I was selected for the role. The position is more of a backend consulting role where I will work with global consulting teams rather than directly with clients, but it offers strong exposure to the consulting domain.

---

## **Deloitte India – Data Scientist**

The interview panel consisted of three members and was quite intense. I was asked deep questions on machine learning and deep learning concepts, including overfitting in models and edge cases like transformers. There were also questions on out-of-bag error and LLM parameters such as temperature.

The questioning style was quite aggressive, and the interview was relatively short. I was not selected after this round.

---

## **TCG Digital – Consultant**

The process began with a test followed by two interview rounds.

In the first round, there was a quick resume-based discussion focusing on my ML internship and concepts like XGBoost. I was also asked about my fraud detection project, including dataset features and evaluation metrics.

The second round involved deeper discussion on my GenAI project where I built a RAG pipeline using LLMs and vector databases. Questions included embedding models, database selection, and pipeline design. There was also discussion about my marketing internship and use of Google Analytics.

The interviews went well, but I did not hear back after getting placed in LEK.

---

## **Overall Reflection**

Overall, my placement journey taught me that while preparation is important, outcomes are influenced by many external factors such as timing, panel preferences, and competition. It is important not to over-prioritize a single company and to stay consistent across all opportunities. In the end, while getting selected in LEK was a great outcome, the learnings from the entire process were far more valuable.
