# Mastercard Consulting – Associate Consultant

The interview process kicked off with a Group Discussion round where we were given various topics to choose from, ranging from big fat Indian weddings to whether India should host the Olympics. My group's topic revolved around evaluating India's current diplomatic situation and whether the country is heading in the right direction. The discussion went well, and I was able to articulate my points clearly.

Moving on to the first technical and resume-based round, the interviewers really grilled me on the machine learning experiences listed on my resume. Since my profile was more tailored towards product roles, I mostly had classical machine learning projects along with a specific case study project. The discussion naturally gravitated towards those topics. During the interview, they gave me an easy puzzle, where the solution involved calculating an infinite geometric progression. The experience took an interesting turn when they asked somewhat random questions, such as explaining the Global Interpreter Lock (GIL) in Python and differentiating between a normal Python list and a NumPy array. At one point, the interviewer was even searching the internet while asking me questions. I managed to answer about 90% of the questions correctly, though I chose to skip the GIL question. I felt confident about advancing to the second round, but unfortunately, I never heard back from them.

Later, I learned from peers that the recruitment strategy seemed skewed; the company initially prioritized interviewing female candidates to fill all available slots, and subsequently selected only three or four male candidates, some of whom were placement representatives. While it felt unfair at the time, this experience taught me a valuable lesson. Placements are heavily dependent on random luck, panel preferences, the time of day you are interviewed, and how well you can sell your story. A single company's decision doesn't define your potential, and Life is simply not always fair.
