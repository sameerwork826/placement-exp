# CBA (Commonwealth Bank of Australia) – AI Engineer / Data Scientist

CBA was the company I primarily targeted and prepared for extensively, and the interview process reflected the depth of my preparation. 

The first round evaluated my foundational skills. I was questioned on version control using Git and GitHub, and solved Data Structures and Algorithms problems involving sorting and Dynamic Programming. We then transitioned to discussing my projects, focusing deeply on vector databases like ChromaDB. The interviewer wanted to know exactly how similarity search works under the hood, alternatives to it, and how Hierarchical Navigable Small World (HNSW) indexing functions. I also answered several SQL questions orally and touched upon core Python Object-Oriented Programming concepts such as abstraction and encapsulation.

The second round was a much more specialized Machine Learning System Design interview tailored to my profile. The interviewer noticed my background and asked how I could help my family's teaching business using AI. I proposed several solutions within a ten-minute window, including using AI for marketing, building a daily operations app for the school, and integrating an LLM-powered chatbot synced with a student database to handle queries from parents. The conversation then shifted to my internship at a non-banking financial company, where I performed contextual loan risk evaluation using LLMs. When I mentioned utilizing LLMs, the interviewer pointed out that CBA's strict rules prohibit the use of public APIs for Personally Identifiable Information (PII). This led to an incredibly illuminating discussion where I learned that CBA completely avoids public APIs like GPT-4, instead opting to run open-source models like Llama entirely on their internal localized networks. We debated the feasibility and cost of this approach, especially anticipating the rapid advancement of model generations. I also took the opportunity to ask them about their operational strategies in India and why they prefer building an in-house Global Capability Center over outsourcing to service giants like TCS or Infosys. The discussion was incredibly engaging, lasting almost an hour before I was instantly pushed to the third round.

The final round was a massive HR and culture-fit interview that stretched out for nearly an hour and a half. I was the very first candidate to give this round. The interviewer asked deep behavioral questions, probing into moments I was proud of, situations I mishandled, and times when I had a conflict with a senior only to later realize they were right. I drew heavily upon my extracurricular experiences as a PR head for an event, to the point where the HR representative eventually asked me to provide academic examples instead. Because the conversation flowed seamlessly, I felt my selection was almost guaranteed. However, after the results were announced, I learned I had not been selected.
