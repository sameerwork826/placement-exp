# BlackRock – Role Unclear (Mix of Data Science/Analyst)

My first round with BlackRock was conducted by two interviewers and focused almost entirely on the practical experiences detailed on my resume. The exact nature of the role remained slightly ambiguous throughout the process, seemingly a blend of software development, AI, and data engineering.

The discussion rapidly zoned in on my contextual loan risk prediction project. The interviewers asked me to walk them through the exact features I used, and I explained my reliance on applicant information alongside credit scoring bureaus. The conversation became heavily focused on banking and finance terminology; we discussed concepts like Non-Performing Assets (NPA), Days Past Due (DPD), and how overall credit card classifications function. They also tested my understanding of the precision-recall trade-off in a realistic business environment. For example, I explained how my model aimed to maximize recall to avoid false negatives, but they challenged me on how generating excessive false positives could negatively impact the business by rejecting too many potential customers. I responded by explaining that minimizing risk was a definitive stakeholder decision in my context, but acknowledged that other metrics like the F1-score could provide a more balanced approach. 

We then transitioned to discussing the integration of Large Language Models within finance. Recognizing the strict data privacy concerns surrounding Personally Identifiable Information (PII) in banking, I suggested utilizing anonymization pipelines and deploying in-house, open-source models trained locally rather than relying on public APIs. I also proposed fine-tuning these models specifically on financial lexicons and leveraging RAG frameworks to adhere strictly to internal company policies and RBI regulations. The interviewers seemed very engaged with these suggestions, and the overall discussion lasted a substantial fifty to sixty minutes. 

Despite an excellent first round, I unfortunately never proceeded to the second. Because I was heavily prioritizing interviews with CBA at the time, I ended up being one of the absolute last candidates to give the first round for BlackRock. By the time my interview finished, the company had already filled the positions with candidates mapped out earlier in the day. This was a critical lesson: over-prioritizing a single company can cost you viable opportunities elsewhere.
