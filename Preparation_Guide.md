# Placement Guidance — Data Science, Analyst, and Product Roles

**A Detailed and Honest Guide by Sameer Wanjari**

## DS Preparation Structure (Based on InfoEdge Syllabus)
This structure is based on the InfoEdge syllabus, which was presented during my placement year. In my opinion, it is one of the best syllabi to follow if you are aiming for core Data Science roles rather than just Analyst positions. 

To start, you should understand the basics of machine learning algorithms deeply. The coding aspect is equally important. I personally relied heavily on StatQuest, Andrew Ng's Machine Learning and Deep Learning notes, and various GitHub resources.

### Maths & Statistics
You must be comfortable with probability and its distributions, sampling, correlation, hypothesis testing, and statistical significance. It is crucial to understand the Central Limit Theorem, paired mean tests, Bayes’ theorem, and maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) along with Maximum a Posteriori (MAP). Linear algebra concepts like Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), eigenvalue decomposition, and matrix operations are also heavily tested.

### Classical Machine Learning & Deep Learning
Ensure you have a firm grasp of bagging, boosting, gradient descent, decision trees, linear and logistic regression, random forests, and gradient boosting machines (XGBoost, LightGBM). You should be familiar with Support Vector Machines (SVM), the kernel trick, regularization, and the bias-variance tradeoff. Mastery over evaluation metrics like precision, recall, F1-score, and True Positive Rate (TPR), along with confusion matrices, missing data imputation, matrix factorization, and density estimation (KDE, K-means) is required. Furthermore, understanding basic NLP (TF-IDF, Word2Vec, FastText, BERT) and neural network architectures ranging from RNNs, CNNs, and LSTMs to Transformers and GANs is highly recommended.

### Programming & Data Handling
Proficiency in Python and SQL is non-negotiable. You must be comfortable using NumPy and Pandas for data reading, handling columns, computing statistical parameters, group-by operations, and executing basic text processing and visualizations.

### Machine Learning Basics (Coding Part)
Be prepared to implement train-test splits, train ML models from scratch, and evaluate model performance dynamically.

## The Importance of CPI
Your CPI matters a lot during shortlisting. Try to maintain a CPI of at least 8 to meet basic eligibility criteria. If you want easier shortlists with comparatively less competitive coding required, aim for a CPI over 9. For context, I sat for placements with an 8.34 CPI.

---

## Online Assessments (OA)
Almost all companies ask aptitude questions. While it may not be at the CAT level, failing to perform well in aptitude will cost you shortlists, especially for analyst roles. For Data Science roles, you might still get shortlisted, but the expectations for Data Structures and Algorithms (DSA) will be significantly higher. 

When I say "good in DSA," I mean you must be comfortable implementing array manipulations, binary search, string operations, and recursion. Dynamic Programming (DP) is also very useful. I highly recommend checking my [Google Sheet](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1UV8QKk-lSBxW8Qpq1aRKDd-D8pF_IyKRSu7EKkN1VFQ/edit?gid=0#gid=0) to see exactly what each company asked in their OAs. Note that very few companies actually come for core Data Science roles, excluding Data Analyst positions.

## Final Reality Check
Ultimately, your placement outcome depends on your CPI, OA performance, the interview panel, your time slot, and sheer luck. You can give a phenomenal interview and still get rejected, or you can give an average one and get selected. Never let placements dictate your self-worth. Your first company is exactly that—a starting point, not your final destination.
